Micro-indicators feed sub-layers. Sub-layers feed layers (and now Tier S). Layers feed the macro score.

Every sub-layer carries two micro-indicators: one categorical fact, one continuous 5-point self-report statement. That's 96 individually-tracked data points across 48 sub-layers — finer resolution, fully traceable top to bottom.

Straight talk: more granularity = better resolution and full traceability, not certainty. This still self-reports on a heuristic framework, not a validated instrument — precision in the math doesn't create precision in human behavior. v6 added 2 sub-layers (Nutrition, Temperament) whose weight ceilings were deliberately reduced below the source paper's implied tier, because independent fact-checking found their supporting citations contested (Danziger 2011) or methodologically split (twin- vs. molecular-genetic heritability) — see Architecture doc Section 3.2b.4. v6.1 added 24 more sub-layers filling every remaining "Not yet implemented" row of that catalogue (excluding Domain 6's protected-attribute rows, which stay deliberately excluded per Section 6). v6.2 gave two of those — Grief and Major life transitions — a proper home: Tier S (Subacute), a 4th scoring tier sitting between the structural layers and the Modulator, matching the timing band (weeks-months) the source paper's Section 3.8.10.9 describes but v5/v6.1's architecture didn't yet model (Section 3.2b.2). Tier S reserves a fixed, undiluted 6% of the score when answered (re-normalized proportionally against the 8 layers above) and flags its own data stale after 35 days — informational only, it keeps counting toward the score even when stale. v6.3 adds Childhood Adversity (ACEs, Felitti et al. 1998) as its own Family & Upbringing sub-layer, at its full HIGH weight — unlike Nutrition/Temperament, this citation was independently confirmed as accurate and one of the best-replicated effects in the whole catalogue, so its weight was not reduced. Treat the final score as a structured mirror, not a verdict.
